# Interview Transcript: Student Member

## Context

- Interview type: Semi-structured stakeholder interview
- Interview date: 2026-05-08
- Interviewer: Business analyst
- Participant: Student member
- Role: University student and regular library user during exam periods
- Current system discussed: Library web application and front desk lending experience

## Transcript

**Interviewer:** Thanks for joining. Could you tell me how you currently use the library?

**Student:** Mostly for study books, sometimes novels, and sometimes just as a place to work. Around exams I borrow more. During the rest of the year, not as much. I also check the catalogue before I come in, but I do not always trust what I see there.

**Interviewer:** What do you mean by not trusting it?

**Student:** A book can look available, but then at the desk it cannot be borrowed. Sometimes it is reference-only. Sometimes someone says it is already out. Sometimes I just get told the system does not allow it. From my side, that feels random.

**Interviewer:** Do staff explain why?

**Student:** They try. I do not want to blame them. Some librarians are really helpful. But it feels like they are also trying to figure it out while I am standing there. If the system blocks something, I expect the system to know why.

**Interviewer:** What is your experience with student pricing?

**Student:** Confusing. We are told students get a free period, which is great. That is one of the reasons I borrow books instead of buying everything. But after that, it gets expensive quickly. It is not just the fine. It is the daily price plus the fine. I only understood that after arguing about a charge once.

**Interviewer:** What happened?

**Student:** I returned a book late. I thought I would pay a small fine. Then the amount was higher than expected. The librarian explained that after the free period ends, I pay the daily price and the overdue fine. Maybe that rule makes sense to someone, but it was not obvious to me before I borrowed the book.

**Interviewer:** Did you know the due date?

**Student:** It was probably on the receipt or in the system, but I did not have a reminder. I do not check library dates every day. I check my phone. If the app or email reminded me, I would return it. It feels unfair to have steep charges and no reminder.

**Interviewer:** How would you want reminders to work?

**Student:** A push notification would be best. Email is okay, but email gets buried. I would want one reminder a few days before the free period ends and maybe one on the due date. If there is going to be a charge after that, make it very clear before it starts.

**Interviewer:** You mentioned an app. Do you use the current web application yourself?

**Student:** Not really. It feels like it is for staff. I can look things up, but I cannot really do much. I would like to see my loans, due dates, and charges myself. I do not want to go to the desk just to ask what I have borrowed.

**Interviewer:** Would you use self-checkout?

**Student:** Definitely, if it worked. I already scan things myself in supermarkets. I would rather borrow a book quickly than wait in line. But I would need to trust it. If I scan a book and then it says no without explaining, that is worse than the desk.

**Interviewer:** What would make self-checkout trustworthy?

**Student:** Show me what is happening. Which member account, which book, due date, and whether it is free or paid. If it cannot be borrowed, say why. Not a technical error code, just something normal. "This is reference-only" or "You already have five active loans." I can handle rules if they are visible.

**Interviewer:** How many books do you normally borrow?

**Student:** It depends. For exams, maybe four or five at once. If there is a limit, I am not against it, but I want to know the limit before I hit it. It is annoying to bring books to the desk and only then discover one cannot be borrowed.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever been blocked because of too many active loans?

**Student:** I think so, but I am not completely sure. That is part of the problem. I was told the loan could not be created. It might have been the limit, or maybe one book was unavailable. I left one book behind because I had a class to get to.

**Interviewer:** What do you like about the current process?

**Student:** When everything works, it is fast. The librarians can create the loan quickly. I like that I do not have to fill in forms. I also like that the library has student pricing at all. Free for the first period is helpful. The problem is that the transition from free to expensive is too invisible.

**Interviewer:** Would clearer pricing make you borrow more?

**Student:** Maybe. If I know exactly when it stops being free and what happens after, I can decide. Right now I avoid borrowing some books because I am afraid I will forget and pay more than the book is worth to me. That is not great for a library, right?

**Interviewer:** How do you feel about different prices for different book types?

**Student:** I would not love that. I understand expensive textbooks are different from novels, but if every category has a different rule, I will stop trying to understand it. Students already compare everything to online alternatives. If the library feels complicated, people will just use PDFs, buy second-hand, or not borrow.

**Interviewer:** What about different membership layers?

**Student:** Like bronze, silver, premium library? That sounds strange. Maybe for events or study rooms, but for books I want simple rules. I am already a student member. If there are more layers, I would need to know what I get. Otherwise it feels like a subscription service.

**Interviewer:** What would you expect in a mobile app?

**Student:** My current loans first. Due dates. Charges. Renew if possible. Reminders. Search books. Maybe reserve a book. And self-checkout if the library wants that. I do not need a beautiful social feed. I need to know whether I am about to owe money.

**Interviewer:** Would you want events and community features?

**Student:** Maybe, but not before the basics. I like the idea of workshops or reading groups, but if the app cannot clearly tell me when a book is due, I do not care about event banners. The library as a community is nice, but the app has to earn trust first.

**Interviewer:** How do you feel when you need staff help?

**Student:** Usually fine. The librarians are nice. But I do not always want to ask. If there is a charge, it is awkward. If I am blocked, it feels like I did something wrong. I would rather see it privately in an app before I am at the desk.

**Interviewer:** Do you think all members would want self-service?

**Student:** No. My grandmother would hate it. She likes talking to the librarian. So I do not think self-checkout should replace people. But students should not have to queue for every simple thing because other members prefer the desk.

**Interviewer:** What is the single biggest improvement you would make?

**Student:** Reminders. If I had to pick one, reminders before the free period ends. Then a clear "what you will pay if late" screen. Then self-checkout. Mobile app somewhere in there, because reminders without mobile are less useful.

**Interviewer:** Anything else?

**Student:** The library should not assume students are trying to avoid paying. Most of us just forget. If the system helped us remember, fewer people would complain. And if we complain less, librarians probably have a better day too.
