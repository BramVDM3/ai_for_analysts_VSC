# Workshop Notes: Regular Members

## Context

- Workshop type: Member experience workshop
- Date: 2026-05-12
- Participants: 3 regular/default members
- Facilitator: Business analyst
- Goal: Understand how regular members experience borrowing, returning, pricing, reminders, and possible future app features
- Note: These are facilitator notes, not a polished transcript. Some comments are paraphrased.

## Participants

- **Member A:** Parent, borrows novels and children's books, visits with two children.
- **Member B:** Working adult, borrows mostly fiction and non-fiction, prefers quick visits.
- **Member C:** Regular reader, also attends occasional author talks and reading group sessions.

## Warm-Up: Current Library Use

Participants described the library as "easy enough" when they already know what they want. All three said staff are helpful and the basic desk process is usually fast.

Member A said the current process works well when borrowing one or two books, but becomes awkward with children selecting several books. They said, "The librarian has to click through each book, and I am trying to keep the children from wandering away."

Member B said they often come during lunch breaks and want to be in and out quickly. They like that the app seems to let staff create loans without much discussion, but they dislike needing to ask staff for basic information like "how many books do I currently have?"

Member C said they like the library's personal atmosphere and do not want the app to make everything feel like a supermarket checkout. They still want better digital information, especially around events and reminders.

## Activity 1: Current Borrowing Journey

The group mapped a typical borrowing journey:

1. Search or browse for books.
2. Bring books to the front desk.
3. Librarian creates loans one by one.
4. Member receives or hears the due date.
5. Member leaves with books.
6. Member tries to remember when to return them.
7. Member returns books.
8. Librarian checks whether anything is owed.

### Observations

- Members do not think in terms of "loans"; they think in terms of "my visit" or "my bag of books".
- The one-book-per-loan structure is invisible to members until it slows down the desk process.
- Participants expected one due-date summary for the whole visit, even if the system stores separate loans.
- Nobody in the group knew the exact maximum number of active loans.
- Member A assumed children’s books might have different rules, but was not sure.
- Member B assumed all regular adult books have the same rules.
- Member C said event registrations and book borrowing feel related because they are both "library things", but not the same process.

## Activity 2: Pricing and Charges

Participants were shown examples of a normal loan, an overdue return, and a student late return. The student rule caused confusion even though none of the participants were students.

Member B said, "If students get it free first and then pay two things after, that should be explained before they borrow, not when they return."

Member A asked whether regular members pay every day or only when late. Another participant answered incorrectly at first, and the group debated it for a few minutes.

Member C said they are not against paying, but dislike "surprise math at the desk".

### Notes From Discussion

- Regular members were less angry about pricing than students, but they still wanted clarity.
- All participants wanted the app or receipt to show the due date clearly.
- Two participants wanted a simple total.
- One participant wanted a breakdown only when there is a charge.
- Member B did not want a complicated screen: "If there is no problem, do not make me read a policy."
- Member C wanted charges explained politely because "money conversations at the desk are uncomfortable."

## Activity 3: Reminders

The group discussed reminders and how they should work.

Member A wanted reminders by email and possibly text message. They said family life makes it easy to forget library dates.

Member B wanted calendar integration. They said, "If it is not in my calendar, it does not exist."

Member C preferred email, but only if the subject line is clear. They said library newsletters are nice, but due-date reminders should not be mixed with event promotion.

### Reminder Preferences

- Reminder before due date: all agreed.
- Reminder on due date: two agreed, one said it might be too late.
- Reminder after due date: all agreed, but tone should not feel threatening.
- Push notifications: only one participant strongly wanted them.
- Printed reminders: Member A said they still help when borrowing with children.

## Activity 4: Self-Service and Mobile

The group was split on self-checkout.

Member B liked the idea immediately. They said, "If I can scan and leave, I would use it."

Member A liked it only if it worked for multiple books at once and did not require too much attention while managing children.

Member C was cautious. They said self-checkout should not remove the desk experience. They also worried that when something goes wrong, the member will still have to go to the librarian anyway.

### Mobile App Discussion

Participants agreed a mobile app could be useful, but they disagreed about priorities.

Member B wanted:

- current loans
- due dates
- renewals
- self-checkout
- calendar integration

Member A wanted:

- family view
- books borrowed by children
- reminders
- one combined return date summary

Member C wanted:

- events
- reading groups
- author talks
- saved interests
- clear borrowing status

The group did not agree whether events should be part of the first mobile version. Member C thought events are central to the library. Member B said, "Please fix borrowing first."

## Activity 5: Blocks and Limits

The facilitator asked what members expect if they cannot borrow another book.

All three said the system should explain why.

Member A said if they have reached a limit, they want to know before standing at the desk with children and books.

Member B said a generic "not allowed" message would make them think the system is broken.

Member C said some explanations should be discreet, especially anything involving debt or suspension.

The group disagreed on how much detail members should see:

- Member B wanted direct, specific explanations.
- Member C wanted softer wording and privacy.
- Member A wanted practical next steps, such as "return one book first" or "ask staff".

## Things Participants Liked

- Staff are helpful and make the process feel personal.
- Basic borrowing is usually quick.
- Seeing books and availability online is useful, even if not always trusted.
- A simple pricing rule is better than "premium-style" membership confusion.
- Events and reading groups are valued, especially by members who see the library as more than lending.

## Pain Points Mentioned

- Members do not know how many books they may borrow.
- Members do not always know how many active loans they currently have.
- One-book-at-a-time loan creation feels slow when borrowing several books.
- Due dates are too easy to forget.
- Pricing and overdue charges feel unclear at the moment they matter.
- Available vs borrowable is not obvious.
- Generic blocked messages would reduce trust.
- Future membership tiers could become confusing.

## Conflicts and Tensions

- Members want self-service, but also want staff available when something goes wrong.
- Some want a very simple screen, while others want detailed breakdowns.
- Events/community features are valuable to some members but feel secondary to others.
- Mobile reminders are useful, but not everyone wants push notifications.
- Clear block explanations are desired, but privacy concerns remain.
- Family borrowing may need a combined view, while the current system appears to treat loans individually.

## Facilitator Summary

Regular members generally like the library and appreciate the current staff-supported process. Their frustration is less intense than the student complaints, but they still experience uncertainty around due dates, borrowing limits, charges, and what "available" means.

The group did not ask for a completely different library experience. They asked for the current experience to become easier to understand, easier to manage, and less dependent on asking staff for basic status information.
