## Context

- Sender: Maria De Smet, Library Director

- Subject: Our library as a community and digital service

## Email

**From:** Maria De Smet, Library Director  
**To:** All library staff  
**Date:** 2026-05-08  
**Subject:** Our library as a community and digital service

Dear colleagues,

Over the past months I have been thinking about the role our library should play in the coming years. I want us to be careful not to define ourselves too narrowly. We are not just a place where books move in and out. We are a learning space, a cultural meeting point, a quiet refuge, a digital gateway, and increasingly a community platform.

The current application has helped us take an important first step. We can now see books, members, loans, and returns in one place. That is a real improvement compared with where we were. I also hear from staff that the basic loan process is relatively simple, which is valuable. We should not lose that.

At the same time, the current system still reflects a very transactional view of the library. A member borrows one book. A member returns one book. A charge is calculated. That is necessary, but it is not the future by itself.

I would like us to explore a broader service model:

- A member-facing mobile experience.
- Better reminders and communication before charges happen.
- Self-service for members who want it.
- Events, lectures, workshops, reading groups, and local community activities.
- More flexible membership models.
- Pricing and lending rules that can support different member groups and different types of material.

This does not mean we abandon personal service. A modern library must be both digital and human. Students may expect self-service and mobile reminders. Some older members may value the desk conversation more than speed. Both needs are valid, and our future services should make room for both.

Pricing is one area where we need to think beyond the current rules. The current model is simple, which is useful, but it is also blunt. Some materials are more in demand, some are expensive to maintain, and some should perhaps remain free or low-cost because they support education. Good pricing should support our mission, not undermine it.

Membership is another area. "Student", "senior", and "default" may be enough for today, but they are not necessarily enough for the library we are becoming. In the future, we may need to support families, researchers, volunteers, schools, donors, or community partners in different ways.

For the next phase, I would like the analysis and product teams to use interviews, complaints, workshops, current data, and system evidence to identify the underlying needs and conflicts. We should expect disagreement. Students, seniors, librarians, product, and IT will not all want the same thing. That disagreement is not a problem; it is part of the work.

Please do not treat the current application as a finished product. Treat it as a foundation. It gives us the first working slice of library operations, but our future app should help us become a more connected, inclusive, and flexible library.

Kind regards,  
Maria
